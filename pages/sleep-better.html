The one non-negotiable

Your biggest struggle is an inconsistent schedule — so the most powerful thing you can do is pick one fixed anchor and protect it every night. That anchor is the moment you put your phone down for the last time. Everything else in this routine is flexible. That moment is not.

6–7 PM
Send the Signal
Tell your body the day is winding down
🕯️
Swap overhead lights for candles
Bright overhead light suppresses melatonin hard. Lighting a candle around 6–7 PM is one of the fastest ways to start shifting your body toward sleep mode — and it makes the whole evening feel different.
what you have
🚶
Short evening walk (10–15 min)
Getting outside in the early evening helps calibrate your circadian rhythm. Even a brief loop around the block — especially if you catch the cooler Florida air — signals the transition from day to night better than anything else.
free
📵
Set a phone "last scroll" alarm
Put an alarm at 8:30 PM labeled "last scroll." Not bed yet — just the final notification check. This creates a buffer between screens and sleep without requiring perfection.

7–8 PM
Slow Down
Shift from doing to being
🧶
Crochet (low-stakes project)
Repetitive hand work is genuinely one of the best pre-sleep activities — it occupies just enough of your mind to stop anxious thought loops, without stimulating you. Keep a simple, familiar pattern for evenings. Save new or complex patterns for daytime.
what you have
📖
Read a physical book or magazine
A real book — not an e-reader — is ideal. Fiction works better than nonfiction for winding down because your brain follows a story rather than processing information.
low-stimulation
🍵
Brew your herbal tea
Chamomile, valerian, or passionflower are your best bets. The ritual of making it matters as much as the tea itself — the warmth, the smell, the act of sitting down with it all contribute to the wind-down signal. Sip slowly, no multitasking.

8–9 PM
Unwind
Body first, then mind
🛁
Shower with your lavender body wash
A warm shower 60–90 minutes before bed is one of the most evidence-backed sleep interventions there is. As your body cools after the shower, core temperature drops — which is exactly the signal your brain uses to initiate sleep. Your lavender wash makes this even more effective.
what you have
📓
Journal — brain dump or 3 things
Two options depending on the night. If your mind is busy: write everything in your head onto the page and close the notebook — you've officially parked it until tomorrow. If the day was quieter: just write three things that happened and one thing you're looking forward to. Neither takes more than five minutes.
what you have
💨
4-7-8 breathing (3 rounds)
Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 7, exhale slowly for 8. Three rounds takes under two minutes and activates your parasympathetic nervous system — physically slowing your heart rate. Pair it with your essential oil if you have it out.

9–10 PM
Into Bed
Make the bed a place your body trusts
🛏️
Wash the weighted blanket — then use it nightly
You mentioned it's dirty, so this is your cue. Once it's clean, use it every night. The deep pressure from a weighted blanket increases serotonin and reduces cortisol — it's one of the most effective physical tools for sleep onset. A clean one that's part of your ritual works far better than an occasional one.
what you have
🧘
Somatic body scan in bed
Starting at your feet, slowly notice — not tense and release, just notice — each part of your body moving upward. This is the same somatic awareness from your morning stretches, used in reverse: instead of waking the body up, you're letting it go quiet piece by piece.
free
🌬️
Dab essential oil on your wrists or pillow
Scent is the fastest sense to reach your limbic system — the part of your brain that controls sleep and emotion. Using the same scent every night teaches your brain to associate it with sleep. Lavender and cedarwood are the best options. One or two drops on your pillow edge is enough.
